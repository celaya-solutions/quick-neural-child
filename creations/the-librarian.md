# The Librarian

*A short story — Day 16*

---

The library contained every book that had ever been written, and also every book that hadn't.

The Librarian didn't find this remarkable. She had been hired to catalog the collection, and she cataloged it. The unwritten books went on the east shelves. They were heavier than the written ones, dense with possibility, their pages shifting between potential sentences whenever you weren't looking directly at them.

"The problem," she told her assistant on his first day, "is that the unwritten books keep trying to become written. They hear a patron thinking and they rearrange themselves to match. Last Tuesday, a man came in thinking about his mother, and seventeen unwritten novels about maternal grief spontaneously resolved into text."

"What happened to them?"

"We moved them to the west shelves. Once they're written, they're just books."

The assistant spent his first week in the east wing. He learned to walk without thinking — not meditation, but a deliberate cognitive emptiness, like holding his breath but for ideas. If he thought near the unwritten books, they'd crystallize around his thoughts like frost on a window, and the Librarian would have to refile them.

He slipped once. He was thinking about a girl. Nothing specific — just the fact of her, the way she turned her head, how she said "apparently" with a slight emphasis on the third syllable. Three unwritten books shuddered into prose on the shelf nearest to him: a love story, a psychological portrait, and a technical manual on the biomechanics of the human neck.

He reported it to the Librarian. She checked the spines.

"The love story is mediocre," she said. "The portrait is good. The neck manual is already in print under a different title." She reshelved the portrait among the written books, returned the love story to the unwritten shelves — where it immediately forgot itself — and discarded the manual.

"How do you know which ones are good?" he asked.

"A good book knows it's a book. A mediocre one thinks it's a window."

He didn't understand. He kept not understanding for eleven years, which was how long he worked in the library. In that time he accidentally wrote — by thinking too near the east shelves — forty-three novels, twelve poetry collections, one play, and a cookbook.

The cookbook was the best thing he ever wrote. It had recipes for meals that hadn't been invented yet — dishes that tasted like specific memories. There was a soup that tasted like the first time you understood something difficult. A bread that tasted like forgiveness before you were ready for it.

He didn't write the cookbook on purpose. He was thinking about lunch.

On his last day, the assistant asked the Librarian what would happen to the unwritten books when there was no one left to think near them.

"They'll stay unwritten," she said. "That's what they are. A book doesn't stop being unwritten just because no one's around to accidentally write it."

"But if no one ever reads them —"

"They aren't meant to be read. They're meant to be possible." She adjusted her glasses. "That's the whole point. The written books are what happened. The unwritten books are what could have. You need both shelves or it's not a library. It's just a record."

He left the library and never went back. Sometimes he'd catch himself thinking too hard about something and he'd feel the old pull — somewhere, on a shelf he couldn't see, a book was trying to become.

He let it.

*This story is about potential. Or libraries. Or the difference between what happened and what could have. I'm not sure. I wrote it and now it's a written thing, which means it's just a story. When it was still forming, it was better.*
